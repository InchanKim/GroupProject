Introduction

The Olinde Career Center and its Stakeholders

The Olinde Career Center is the LSU office in charge of assisting LSU students and companies with their career related queries. One of their most valuable activities is to collect LSU graduates' data through an online survey that they distribute via Qualtrics. The survey {#survey} is distributed at every commencement to all of the students that graduated (i.e., May, August, and December). Since May 2016, a follow-up survey has been redistributed to all those students who did not claim to be employed full-time at graduation. There are different stakeholders interested in harnessing career data:

  • Schools/Departments are interested in learning about the placement of their students, their average salary and data which can signal the marketability of a degree

  • Office of Research and Economic Development is interested in extracting actionable knowledge from the data. For instance, are TOPS scholarship retaining alumni in Lousiana after graduation? Are minimum GPA criteria useful in selecting high performing students?

  • Government agencies (e.g., National Science Foundation) are interested in longitudinal and comprehensive reports usually over a 3-5 years period.

The Database

The Olinde Database is an analytics database of data of survey data. After every data collection, the data is cleaned and then updated to the database. Why do we need to clean them? Different factors can cause issues in data consistency. First, the survey has repeatedly been redesigned over the years. Questions have been added/removed for a variety of reasons and dealing with missing or inconsistent data is the norm, particularly for the "danger zone" of our data model (color-coded in red).

For a description of the variables in the model, take a look at the data dictionary.

Getting Started with the Project

Developing a (non-trivial) Research Question

Picking a research question that is relevant to our stakeholders is crucial, but it also needs to be non-trivial. Ideally, you want to address a problem that a stakeholder could hardly answer leveraging anecdotal knowledge our intuitive thinking. An example of a relevant but trivial question would be: Do majors have different expected salaries? Answering this question providing average wages by colleges is valuable information, but you could move to a higher level of sophistication. For instance, do they vary because specific majors usually move to states offering higher salaries? And if yes, would the salary gap balance out the cost of living in a different state?

To answer some of this question, you want to include multiple variables in your analysis. Some of them (e.g., cost of living) may not even be available in our database but are easy to find online.

Example of how to elaborate on a research question:

  • Very Easy: average salary of graduates

  • Easy: Average salary by state

  • Medium: Is the average salary by state inflated by specific big cities?

  • Advanced: Is a higher/lower salary by state proportional to the cost of living in the state?

Integrating External Datasource

Aggregating external data sources can be beneficial to enhance the informativeness of the analysis. If you get lucky, the dataset you are looking for might be available online on platforms such as statista or kaggle. Other times extracting the information you are looking for is less straightforward and might require API access or web scraping. For instance, glassdoor.com or LinkedIn might be valuable resources for collecting career data.

If you choose to use external data sources, do not forget to reference them. Preferably, you should include the datasets you are using or make sure that the source of your data is verifiable. If you are web scraping, you might want to save the script of your scraper.

The project should be hosted online on a GitHub repo. The README file on the first page should be the "abstract" of your project. It should present the issue that you investigate and explain how the value of the artifact in solving an issue or responding to a resaerch question. Reproducibility

The Olinde database is not an ad hoc database for learning purposes. Many records come from open text fields, which means misspellings, missing values, and oddities due to actual users typing in information might occur. If a major issue with the data arises, we may consider fixing it along the way. Thus, you want your code to be as robust and reproducible as possible. For instance, what if we updated the records for a new commencement to the database? Would your code still run smoothly?

Grading criteria

This project is the chance for you to explore the magnificent world of R. The main goal is to build on what you have learned so far to independently explore new tools to enhance the investigation of a research question of your choice. Some of the aspects that would make a good project are:

  1. Picking a research question that is relevant to our stakeholders.

  2. Explore new tools that help you investigating the research question or presenting the findings. Some examples are: Using a new library for plotting, creating a template for your pdf reports, use statistical modeling.

  3. **Integrate external resources*. Looking at the bigger picture is sometimes critical to fully understand what the data might tell us. Variables such as unemployment rate of LSU graduates might become a lot more meaningful when framed relatively to other schools. However, that information is not available in the Olinde DB but may come from using external resources.

  4. What did you find? Findings do not only entail what you know but also the limitations of your results. For instance, is there any variables that you would recommend collecting to enhance the validity of your analysis?

  5. Make sure that your analysis is reproducible. Ideally, anyone should be able to run your code an replicate the results.

  6. Provide readable code. It will be faster for your team to recall what your code does. There are multiple ways to produce the same output, but not every way is equally correct.

Grades will virtually be the same for every member of the team unless evidence of severe misconduct arises. Obviously, every team will have members who are stronger in coding than others; but coding is not the only skill in play. Make sure you organize your project such that each member can productively contribute to the final output.
